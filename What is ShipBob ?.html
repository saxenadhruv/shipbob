What is a 3PL ?

  • 3PL is short for third party logistics providers. Consumer brands sell physical products to consumers through three main channels :
  1. Physical brick and mortar retailers like WholeFoods, BestBuy, Macys etc. or
  2. Online retailers like marketplaces - Amazon, Walmart, Ebay
  3. Online direct to the customer through their website - Referred to as D2C ( Direct to Consumer)

Across all these three channels, brands need a warehouse to store their physical products ( inventory ) and ship their products to either the retailers ( channel 1 and channel 2) or directly to their customers ( channel 3). Instead of building their own warehousing, shipping and distribution capabilities, they can outsource these services to a 3PL to warehouse and ship on their behalf.

3PLs generally provide these services to brands :

  • Inbound : Receiving and warehousing a brand’s products from the brand’s manufacturer
  • Outbound:
    • Pallet / Freight Shipping : Shipping pallets to physical and online retailers (sales channel 1 and 2) ( what is a pallet)
      • E-commerce / D2C shipping : Shipping individual orders ( called eaches - since each order is unique for every end customer

Most 3PLs started in the B2B world and specialize in doing inbound and outbound - pallet / freight shipping for channels 1 and 2. This is because Outbound e-commerce/ D2C shipping for orders originating on the brand’s website is a relatively new sales channel for most brands and the physical and software infrastructure needed inside the 3PLs to ship individual orders looks very different and is much more complex than shipping pallets to retailers.

ShipBob specializes in shipping eaches for direct to consumer brands

Why is shipping “eaches “ for e-commerce different from shipping “pallets” for retailers?


Lets walk through a quick example. Physical retailers like Walmart purchase products from brands in large quantities. Let’s pretend you are brand Charmin selling Toilet Paper and uses a 3PL to warehouse and ship orders to retailers and Walmart is the retailer.


A typical retailer’s B2B order

  • Walmart creates a purchase order for Charmin to ship 10,000 units of Charmin Toilet Paper to Walmart’s distribution center. This is a typical B2B ( Business to Business) order where you ship large quantities of items to a business address.
  • Inside this 3PL , this toilet paper is stored on pallets.
    • Each pallet contains 500 units of toilet paper. See photo of such a 3PL below.

a 3PL storing pallets to ship B2B orders


  • The 3PL will prepare 20 pallets each containing 500 units of toilet paper and schedule a freight truck to grab those 20 pallets.
  • The freight truck belonging to a local trucking company or to UPS/ FedEx Freight shows up at the scheduled time and picks up the 20 pallets from the 3PL .
  • The 3PL associate signs a physical BOL ( Bill of Lading) as proof of shipment to the freight company and upload the signed copy to Walmart’s system and is done.

Essentially for the 3PL - its receives a pallet in and sends a pallet out.


Now let’s compare it to a D2C order.

An online Direct to Consumer Order

  • Charmin also sells online through its website ( charmin.com) and sells toilet paper and kitchen towel. You as a consumer buy 6 units of Toilet paper and 6 units of Kitchen Towel
  • This 3PL is connected into the website’s backend to get notified when the order is placed. To fulfill the order :
    • Picking :
  1. the 3PL employee has to go to the pallet location containing the Toilet paper, break the pallet and pick 6 units of Toilet Paper.
  2. Then the employee walks over to the Kitchen Towel aisle (which could be at the other end of the FC) and picks 6 units of the towel.

  • A ShipBob picker picking e-commerce orders

  • Packaging
  1. Then both of these items are given to a packer who has to put them together in a packaging box with some packing material to keep the items safe during transit.
  • Labeling
  1. Then the order makes its way through to the labeling station where a person or a machine applies a shipping label to the order. The shipping label can belong to either UPS, USPS , FedEx ( Called Last Mile Carriers ) dependent on how quickly the brand wants the order to arrive at their customer’s doorstep.
  2. The last mile carriers shows up at their regular scheduled time in the evening and grab the package from the 3PL and get it en-route for delivery.
  3. The tracking number is then uploaded back to Charmin’s website to communicate to the end consumer.

Comparing an online D2C order vs a B2B order ( Business to Business ) , the complexity of a D2C order is significantly higher than a B2B order :


  1. An D2C order contains multiple types of products and lower quantities of each product.
  2. A picker’s walking route inside the FC must be optimized to reduce travel time inside the FC.
  • So it might make sense to intelligently store some units of the Toilet paper and Kitchen Towel in an easy to pick location and also place them close to one another so they can be grabbed easily as they are ordered frequently together.


A ShipBob fulfillment center storing items in bins


  1. The packer must use the right box to package the shipment as it affects the price of the shipment. Last Mile carriers (UPS, FedEx) charge the 3PL for shipping based on the weight and size of the box. So the box dimensions used for the shipment are important.
  2. The decision to choose the last mile carrier must be made correctly to account for delivery time as chosen by the consumer who placed the order on the website.
  3. The inventory for the toilet paper and the kitchen towel must be accounted for correctly as you are picking individual units instead of picking entire pallets in multiples of 500.
  4. The unique tracking number must be uploaded back to the website for the consumer to know how to track the order.

Looking at the difference between the two channels :

  • A B2B 3PL which has optimized for Retail B2B orders will have to retro-fit their physical and software infrastructure to this new online channel which is dramatically different from their existing B2B capabilities.

  • Physical infrastructure of Pallet Storage needs to be converted to shelves and bins to store smaller quantities of items.

Since a B2B 3PL was primarily optimized for pallet storage - they are located in rural areas where they can occupy a large real estate footprint to store pallets. A D2C FC instead optimized for transit time needs to be located close to the population hubs of the country.


  • Commonly ordered items need to be stored closer to each other.
  • Picking carts and pack stations need to be built to pick and pack individual items.

  • The way B2B 3PLs have responded to the growing e-commerce channel is by selecting a few number of large merchants that they can service well for both B2B and D2C and building a customized infrastructure for each one of those merchants.

So a typical 3PL will bring on 15-20 large volume merchants in a year and create processes customized for them which takes a 8-10 week on-boarding cycle. ShipBob on the other hand has built a platform focusing on D2C brands. Hence we are able to bring in 300 merchants/quarter to service their e-commerce orders at scale as we have built our entire infrastructure to support D2C orders only.


So how is ShipBob different from existing 3PLs?


ShipBob is focused on direct to consumer e-commerce fulfillment. We have built a full stack solution from the ground up where software and hardware ( Physical Fulfillment centers) work in sync to provide a great e-commerce fulfillment solution for direct to consumer brands.



Layer 1 : Merchant Application : This is the communication layer and an ERP layer for merchants/brands to run their back office operations. They connect all their direct to consumer sales channels into this merchant application and their teams login every day to ensure their back office operations are running smoothly.

  • Merchant Application

Layer 2 : Warehouse Operating System : This is the brains of the fulfillment network. This software for example decides which FC in the network to allocate the order to, inside the FC - what parallel process paths/sequence to pick and pack the orders for the day inside the FC, which box to use for packaging the order, which last mile carrier to use etc.

  • Warehouse Operating System

Layer 3 : The Fulfillment network : This is the physical Fulfillment centers. The ShipBob Warehouse Operating System is deployed inside the Fulfillment centers

  • We have 4 FCs that we lease and operate. Operating these FCs has made us subject matter experts in at-scale e-commerce fulfillment.
  • We have added 5 more FCs in our network which are not owned/operated by us but think of them as franchise locations. They follow the same processes and utilize the ShipBob Warehouse Operating System to process the orders for ShipBob merchants.

  • ShipBob Fulfillment Centers

Why did we decide to build the full stack solution instead of just building one layer of the stack - like just the software layer ?


  1. First Principles Thinking : E-commerce fulfillment is different. Instead of working with legacy 3PLs who have added an e-commerce module on top of their existing B2B capabilities - we took this opportunity to design the entire fulfillment stack for e-commerce - which is the future. This is reflected in the differentiated experience merchants have on ShipBob.
  2. Small to mid sized independent brands are the future of e-commerce : We wanted to build the infrastructure to service thousands of brands under one network instead of focusing on a smaller number of large brands. This is contrarian to how most 3PLs think.
  3. Operational Leverage : Logistics is at scale business. As we make improvements to our Merchant Application and Warehouse Operating system it improves our FC labor productivity this reduces our operating costs these gains accrue back to us as a company. This growing operational leverage builds a at-scale moat for us as a company.

How are we able to provide brands with a Prime like experience ?


  • ShipBob allows brands to offer 2 Day, next day and (future state) same day delivery experience to their customers across the country.

We do this without using UPS Overnight or UPS 2 day but actually using ground shipping.


  • Ground shipping is the cheapest form of shipping ( For example USPS - First Class is $4 and on USPS website offers 3-7 business day transit time estimate and is not a guaranteed 2 day delivery service . However by building :
  1. Probabilistic time in transit models which forecast what carrier to use for that particular destination zip code and
  2. Smart distribution of inventory across a network of FCs across the country

we are able to re-use USPS First Class as a guaranteed 2 day delivery service.


  • The alternative method of offering 2 day shipping is by offering UPS guaranteed 2 day for $15-$20 which is extremely expensive.

  • This is how Amazon Prime works - They are able to offer free shipping by smart allocation of inventory - and we are replicating it for Direct to Consumer brands.
